# Rowhammer Mitigation: From Streaming, Hybrid, and In-DRAM Approaches to Open Root of Trust

Modern hardware security is no longer just a story about software checks layered on top of reliable components. It is increasingly a story about physical limits, controller policy, timing slack, redundancy budgets, and trust boundaries that have to hold even when the workload is adversarial. DRAM disturbance, metadata placement, ECC design, and root-of-trust engineering all now sit inside the same larger problem: how to preserve isolation when the hardware itself is under stress.

That shift matters because the old assumptions are weakening at the same time. Rowhammer thresholds are falling, blast radii are widening, memory controllers are running out of cheap state, and server platforms want reliability and security metadata in the same narrow codeword. At the platform level, users are also less willing to accept opaque trust anchors that can only be evaluated through vendor claims. The result is a more architectural view of security. The question is no longer just whether a mechanism exists, but whether it remains safe under worst-case timing, worst-case access patterns, and the full lifecycle of the device.

Five design directions illustrate that change especially well: bounded streaming trackers for Rowhammer, hierarchical tracking for ultra-low thresholds, activation-coupled in-DRAM refresh, bit-efficient ChipKill via residue codes, and OpenTitan's open root-of-trust architecture. They address different layers of the stack, but they share the same design instinct: make the dangerous behavior explicit, bound it mathematically, and give the hardware a direct way to respond before the failure becomes visible.

# 1. Rowhammer Mitigation in Memory Systems

# 1.1 Streaming-Based Rowhammer Tracking in the Memory Controller

Rowhammer begins with an ordinary property of DRAM. A DRAM cell stores information as charge in a tiny capacitor, and that charge leaks over time, which is why the device must refresh rows periodically. The problem is that repeated row activation is not electrically neutral. Every `ACT` operation stresses nearby structures, and at modern feature sizes repeated activations can disturb adjacent or near-adjacent rows enough to flip their bits.

That makes Rowhammer more than a reliability bug. It becomes a failure of isolation. Software should not be able to corrupt memory it does not own simply by driving the memory system hard enough, yet that is exactly what repeated activation can achieve. The practical attack surface is defined by the DRAM timing envelope. If $t_{REFW}$ is the refresh window, $t_{REFI}$ the refresh interval, $t_{RFC}$ the refresh command time, and $t_{RC}$ the row cycle time, then the maximum number of activations that fit inside one observation window is

$$
W = \frac{t_{REFW}(1 - t_{RFC}/t_{REFI})}{t_{RC}}
$$

This quantity `W` is important because any tracking mechanism must be dimensioned for the worst case, not the average case. If a bank can sustain that many activations inside one refresh window, then a secure defense must assume an attacker can attempt exactly that.

Another key parameter is the Row Hammer threshold `TRH`. This is the number of activations an aggressor row can accumulate before nearby victim rows begin to experience bit flips. Older generations of DRAM tolerated larger activation counts. Newer generations tolerate less, which means the margin between normal access behavior and dangerous access behavior keeps shrinking.

## Rowhammer as a Failure of Isolation

Row Hammer is not just a reliability issue. It is a violation of isolation. Software that does not directly own a memory row should not be able to corrupt it simply by activating adjacent or nearby rows often enough. Once that becomes possible, memory protection guarantees no longer depend only on address translation and permission bits. They also depend on the physical behavior of the memory array.

In the simplest case, a single aggressor row disturbs one or two immediate neighbors. In practice, the situation can be more complicated. Disturbance may propagate beyond the nearest rows, producing non-adjacent effects such as `+/-2` or `+/-3` victims. The strength of the disturbance usually decays with distance, but it does not always vanish quickly enough to ignore. This is why many modern defenses are designed around the broader idea of disturbance management, not just adjacency detection.

The attack model is also adversarial rather than statistical. A normal workload might never come close to the activation threshold, but a carefully crafted sequence of accesses can intentionally maximize row activations within one refresh window. That makes hardware efficiency a hard requirement. A defense that only works for average workloads is not a defense.

## Probabilistic vs. Deterministic Defenses

Most Row Hammer mitigations fall into two broad categories.

The first category is probabilistic protection. In these schemes, the controller occasionally refreshes nearby victim rows when it sees activity that might be suspicious. The appeal is obvious: the hardware is simple, and exact row-by-row counting can be avoided. The weakness is equally obvious: a probability is not a guarantee. If an attacker can keep hammering long enough, a low-probability failure eventually becomes a real failure. Raising the refresh probability improves security, but it also raises refresh traffic, energy cost, and performance overhead.

The second category is deterministic tracking. Here, the controller attempts to identify high-activity rows before they cross a dangerous threshold. This is more attractive from a security standpoint because it can provide a strict bound on undetected activity. The challenge is scale. Exact tracking across large DRAM structures can require many counters, large tables, complicated update logic, and more area than vendors are willing to spend.

This tradeoff is what motivates algorithmic approaches that do not count every row exactly, but still guarantee that the dangerous rows will be caught.

## Why Streaming Algorithms Fit the Problem

A useful insight is that row activations can be treated as a data stream. Each ACT command contributes one item, and that item is simply the activated row address. From that perspective, an aggressor row is a heavy hitter: a value that appears unusually often in a bounded stream.

This is where frequent-element algorithms become relevant. Instead of storing a precise counter for every possible row, a hardware tracker can keep a small summary of the stream and still retain strong guarantees about high-frequency elements. The classical Misra-Gries algorithm is especially appealing because it is simple, deterministic, and space efficient. It maintains a fixed number of tracking entries and guarantees that any item appearing often enough must be represented in the summary.

For Row Hammer protection, that guarantee can be expressed as a table-sizing rule:

$$
N_{entry} > \frac{W}{T - 1}
$$

Here, $N_{entry}$ is the number of tracking entries, `W` is the maximum number of activations in the observation window, and `T` is the internal alert threshold. The meaning of the inequality is practical: if the table is this large, then any row activated at least `T` times cannot disappear from the tracker.

That is the core reason heavy-hitter algorithms fit this problem so well. They do not waste hardware on cold rows. They reserve guaranteed visibility for hot rows, which are exactly the rows that matter for Row Hammer.

## Safe Estimation With Spillover Accounting

A lightweight hardware tracker usually keeps more than just addresses. It also maintains estimated counts and a small amount of shared metadata that captures unassigned activity. One effective pattern is to use a table of `(row address, estimated count)` pairs plus a global spillover count.

When an activation hits an entry already in the table, the estimated count is incremented. When the activated row is not present, the tracker looks for a cold entry whose count matches the spillover value. If one exists, that entry is replaced by the new row, and the row inherits a starting count of `spillover + 1`. If no such entry exists, the spillover count itself is incremented.

This replacement rule is subtle but important. A newly inserted row is not assumed to have zero prior activity. It inherits a conservative estimate based on how much stream activity has passed through the system without dedicated representation. That bias makes the tracker safer because it prefers overestimation to underestimation.

A simple controller-side update routine can be expressed as follows:

<details><summary>Code</summary>

```python
def process_activation(activated_addr):
    if i := ROW_ADDR_CAM.SEARCH(activated_addr):
        incremented_cnt = COUNT_CAM.READ(i) + 1
        COUNT_CAM.WRITE(i, incremented_cnt)
    else:
        if i := COUNT_CAM.SEARCH(SPCNT):
            incremented_cnt = SPCNT + 1
            ROW_ADDR_CAM.WRITE(i, activated_addr)
            COUNT_CAM.WRITE(i, incremented_cnt)
        else:
            SPCNT += 1
```

</details>

This code example illustrates the three essential cases in a lightweight tracker: a direct hit on an already tracked row, a replacement of a cold entry whose count matches the spillover state, and a pure spillover increment when no entry can be recycled safely.

The desired invariant is

$$
\text{Estimated Count}(X) \ge \text{Actual Count}(X)
$$

for any row `X` that is currently being tracked. If this invariant holds, the controller never misses danger by counting too low. At worst, it acts a little early.

The spillover term must also be bounded:

$$
\text{Spillover Count} \le \frac{W}{N_{entry} + 1}
$$

This ensures that untracked activity cannot grow without limit. Even if some accesses are temporarily absorbed into spillover rather than assigned to explicit entries, the amount of uncertainty remains controlled by the table size.

## Converting Algorithmic Limits Into Safety Margins

An internal threshold `T` only becomes meaningful when it is tied back to the physical Row Hammer threshold `TRH`. That link is not one-to-one, because the controller must account for adversarial effects that make the physical limit more dangerous than it first appears.

One issue is the double-sided pattern, where a victim row is disturbed by two aggressors instead of one. Another issue is window alignment. If the tracker resets at refresh-window boundaries, an attacker may accumulate activity near the end of one window and continue near the start of the next. A safe design therefore acts before the physical threshold is even close to being reached.

One conservative bound is

$$
2(T - 1) < \frac{TRH}{2}
\Rightarrow
T < \frac{TRH}{4} + 1
$$

This inequality captures a practical engineering principle: the controller's alert threshold must be substantially lower than the raw device threshold if it is expected to remain safe under worst-case access alignment and multi-sided disturbance.

For non-adjacent disturbance, the threshold must be reduced further. If the relative impact on rows at distances `2` through `n` is modeled by coefficients $\mu_2, \ldots, \mu_n$, then the safe threshold becomes

$$
T < \frac{TRH}{4(1 + \mu_2 + \cdots + \mu_n)} + 1
$$

This is a clean example of how physical behavior and algorithmic policy meet. Better physical modeling leads directly to better threshold selection.

## Practical Hardware Support for the Tracker

A theoretical summary structure is only useful if it can be implemented fast enough for a real memory controller. That is where hardware organization matters.

One common choice is to use content-addressable memory, or CAM. An Address CAM can determine whether the activated row is already being tracked, while a Count CAM can locate entries whose counts match the spillover value and are therefore suitable replacement candidates. CAM is well suited to this task because the controller needs content-based lookup rather than index-based lookup.

Another useful optimization is the overflow bit. A Row Hammer tracker does not always need a wide exact counter. Once the internal threshold region is reached, the only important fact may be that the count has crossed the alert boundary. Replacing part of the counter width with a simple overflow indicator reduces area without weakening the protection logic.

Memory-controller support is equally important. Detection logic must be paired with a mitigation path that can refresh likely victim rows near an aggressor. This is often described as a Nearby Row Refresh capability or an equivalent controller-to-DRAM interface. Without such a mechanism, the controller might know that a row is dangerous but lack an efficient way to neutralize the risk.

An additional advantage of controller-based tracking is that it can be more robust to internal DRAM address remapping. If the controller reasons at the row-address level and delegates the actual nearby-row refresh through a supported interface, it avoids baking fragile physical-layout assumptions into the tracker itself.

## Window Size as a Security-Area Tradeoff

Resetting the tracking table once per full refresh window is not the only option. Some designs shrink the tracker window to $t_{REFW}/k$, which reduces the number of activations observed before the table is cleared. This can lower the required table size, but it also changes the safety bound because resets happen more often.

The adjusted threshold becomes

$$
T < \frac{TRH}{2(k + 1)} + 1
$$

and the corresponding entry requirement can be written as

$$
N_{entry} > \frac{2 \times 1360K}{TRH} \cdot \frac{k + 1}{k - 1}
$$

The lesson here is broader than the specific formula. Shorter windows save space, but they also make the design more conservative, which can increase unnecessary refreshes. This is a standard hardware tradeoff: less state often means more intervention. A good design chooses `k` carefully rather than assuming that smaller is always better.

## Design Requirements for Effective Rowhammer Defense

From a systems perspective, an effective Row Hammer defense must satisfy several constraints at once.

It must be secure under adversarial access patterns rather than only on benchmark averages. It must fit within realistic controller timing and area budgets. It must tolerate continuing technology scaling, including lower activation thresholds and a wider disturbance radius. And it must preserve normal-case performance, because a defense that constantly injects extra refreshes will be difficult to deploy no matter how safe it is.

This is why the combination of DRAM timing analysis, disturbance modeling, streaming heavy-hitter detection, CAM-based lookup, and targeted refresh support is so compelling. These pieces reinforce one another. Timing analysis sets the worst-case stream length. Disturbance modeling determines how conservative the threshold must be. The streaming algorithm identifies the hot rows without full counting. CAM makes the summary searchable at controller speed. Nearby-row refresh turns detection into action.

# 1.2 Hybrid Tracking for Ultra-Low Rowhammer Thresholds

The general tracking problem becomes much harsher once the physical threshold falls into the low hundreds. With $T_{REFW} = 64 ms$ and $t_{RC}$ near `45 ns`, a bank can issue roughly

$$
N_{act} = \frac{T_{REFW}}{t_{RC}} \approx 1.36 \times 10^6
$$

activations inside one refresh window. If the Rowhammer threshold is only `500`, then the controller has to separate dangerous from harmless behavior inside an activation space that is orders of magnitude larger than the safety margin. That breaks many conventional tracking assumptions.

Exact per-row tracking in SRAM is too large, because the controller would need counters for every row in memory. DRAM-resident metadata scales better in capacity, but it is too slow if every activation must update a counter in memory. A practical low-threshold design therefore has to be approximate first and exact only when necessary.

## Hybrid Tracking With GCT, RCT, and RCC

This is the logic behind hybrid tracking with a Group-Count Table (GCT), a Row-Count Table (RCT), and a Row-Count Cache (RCC).

The GCT is an SRAM-based filter that counts activity at row-group granularity rather than per row. This gives it two advantages. First, it is small enough to remain on chip. Second, its access latency is low enough to keep up with the memory controller's hot path. The GCT does not try to identify the exact aggressor immediately. Its role is to screen out the vast majority of groups whose activity never becomes dangerous.

When a group's aggregate activity becomes suspicious, tracking shifts to the RCT. The RCT stores per-row counters in DRAM address space, so it can scale to full-memory coverage without consuming large amounts of SRAM. Exact per-row accounting now becomes possible, but only for the limited set of rows that have already survived the first-stage filter.

Because DRAM-resident metadata would otherwise introduce too much latency, the design includes an RCC, a small SRAM cache for recently used RCT entries. The RCC absorbs repeated accesses to hot metadata and prevents the per-row tracker from degenerating into constant DRAM lookups. In this sense, the hybrid design does more than split storage. It deliberately places coarse filtering, exact tracking, and metadata locality at different levels of the hierarchy.

In a representative configuration, the design uses a 32K-entry GCT and an 8K-entry RCC, for a total SRAM footprint of about 56 KB. That is small enough to be realistic for a controller while still giving the system enough on-chip state to screen most activity before touching DRAM metadata.

## Promotion Paths and Exact Per-Row Counting

The tracker uses two thresholds. $T_G$ is the group threshold that governs when coarse tracking is no longer sufficient, and `T` is the per-row mitigation threshold that governs when defensive action must be taken.

As long as a row group's counter remains below $T_G$, the access stays entirely within the GCT fast path. This is the normal case. The metadata cost is a small SRAM update, and no per-row state is needed.

Once the group counter reaches $T_G$, the relevant rows are promoted into the RCT. Their counters are initialized to $T_G$, and every subsequent activation is counted exactly. That initialization rule is intentionally conservative. A specific row might not be responsible for all of the group's recorded activity, but starting from $T_G$ ensures the transition never loses information. The tracker may overestimate, but it will not underestimate.

The GCT's aggregate filtering capacity can be summarized as

$$
C_{GCT} = N_{entries} \times T_G
$$

With 32,768 entries and $T_G = 200$, the table can absorb about 6.4 million activations before all counters saturate. This is large enough to filter a substantial fraction of ordinary traffic and keep the exact tracker off the critical path most of the time.

## Why the Internal Threshold Must Lead the Physical Limit

A subtle difficulty arises because tracking structures are reset periodically. If an attacker issues `T - 1` activations just before a reset and another `T - 1` just after it, then the effective amount of hammering that can occur without immediate mitigation is

$$
T_{eff} = 2 \times T - 1
$$

This means the internal mitigation threshold cannot be set equal to the physical Row-Hammer threshold. To protect a device with vulnerability threshold $N_{RH}$, the tracker instead sets

$$
T \le \frac{N_{RH}}{2}
$$

For a target of $N_{RH} = 500$, the safe internal value is therefore `T = 250`. This early trigger is not wasted margin. It is what prevents split-window hammering from slipping through the reset boundary.

## Why the Security Argument Uses Conservative Bounds

The security argument relies on one property above all others: the tracker never undercounts activations.

During the coarse-tracking phase, a row's true activation count is bounded above by the group counter. When promotion happens,

$$
T_G \ge ACT_{Phase1}
$$

where $ACT_{Phase1}$ is the row's actual number of activations before promotion. Initializing the row's exact counter to $T_G$ is therefore safe because it starts from a value that is at least as large as the real count.

After promotion, counting is exact. If mitigation occurs at threshold `T`, then

$$
T = T_G + ACT_{Phase2}
$$

where $ACT_{Phase2}$ is the number of exact activations observed after promotion. Combining the two phases yields a simple conclusion: mitigation cannot occur later than the safe bound implied by the chosen threshold. In other words, the mechanism may trigger a little early, but it does not trigger too late.

This conservative accounting also helps against more adaptive attacks. If disturbance effects such as Half-Double require additional victim-related activity to be considered, those events can be incorporated into the aggressor accounting. The metadata itself must also be protected, since counters stored in DRAM could become hammer targets. A small dedicated SRAM structure is enough to track accesses to the rows holding tracking metadata and prevent that loophole from becoming the weak point in the system.

## Why the Hybrid Architecture Remains Fast

The performance argument is straightforward: exact per-row tracking is expensive only if it becomes frequent. The whole purpose of the GCT and RCC is to make that rare.

In evaluated configurations, the GCT filters about 90.7 percent of row activations. Most accesses therefore end after a cheap SRAM update. Of the remaining accesses, many hit in the RCC, which avoids another round trip to DRAM metadata. Only a small tail falls through to the RCT in memory. This is why the design can retain precise tracking where needed without paying the full DRAM-metadata penalty on every activation.

The reported overheads reflect that hierarchy. At a target threshold of 500, average slowdown is only about 0.7 percent. Even at a much more aggressive threshold of 125, the average slowdown remains around 4 percent. These numbers show that the architecture scales much better toward future low-threshold devices than approaches that depend entirely on large SRAM tables or frequent DRAM metadata updates.

The same logic explains why each component is necessary. Without the GCT, too many accesses would immediately require precise tracking. Without the RCC, exact tracking would generate too much metadata traffic. The design works because the first stage filters broadly, the second stage counts precisely, and the cache keeps the precise stage from dominating latency.

## Architectural Contribution of the Hybrid Design

The real contribution of the hybrid approach is not a single structure, but a clean division of labor.

SRAM handles the common case because it is fast and well suited to aggregate filtering. DRAM handles the rare case because it is large enough to hold full per-row metadata. The cache sits between them to recover locality from hot counter accesses. The thresholds connect these structures to the security argument, ensuring that promotion and mitigation happen early enough to preserve safety even around refresh-window boundaries.

This combination is what makes the design practical at ultra-low thresholds. It accepts that exact tracking for all rows is too expensive, and it also accepts that approximate tracking alone is not sufficient. The solution is to be approximate first, exact second, and conservative throughout.

# 1.3 Activation-Coupled In-DRAM Refresh for Rowhammer Protection

Tracking hot rows is not the only way to defend against Rowhammer. Another possibility is to shift more of the work into DRAM itself and use the act of opening a row as an opportunity to perform preventative maintenance.

## Why Rowhammer Is Becoming Harder to Contain

Rowhammer is best understood through two scaling trends.

The first is the shrinking Rowhammer threshold $R_{thresh}$. This is the maximum number of aggressor-row activations that a victim row can tolerate before disturbance causes a bit flip. Older devices could withstand far more activations than newer ones. As $R_{thresh}$ decreases, the memory system has less time to respond before corruption becomes possible.

The second is the growth of the blast diameter `B`. In the classical picture, one aggressor row disturbs only its immediate neighbors, so `B = 2`. In newer devices, disturbance can propagate further, affecting rows at larger offsets and making the effective victim set wider. This means a protection scheme can no longer assume that only one row on each side matters.

These trends are especially problematic for tracking-based defenses. If the controller or DRAM must explicitly identify “hot” aggressors, then lower thresholds demand faster responses and larger blast diameters demand broader victim coverage. The result is more state, more policy complexity, and more opportunities for attacker-crafted access patterns to stress the tracking logic itself.

That is why a different approach becomes attractive: instead of predicting which rows need help, generate refresh work regularly in proportion to how often the sub-array is being exercised.

## Why Accurate DRAM Models Matter

Any proposal that repurposes internal DRAM timing lives or dies by circuit realism. A simplified timing diagram is not enough, because the relevant constraints arise from transistor sizing, bitline capacitance, sub-word line delay, and I/O loading.

At a circuit level, a row activation moves through four recognizable phases:

1. Precharge equalizes the bitlines around a reference level, typically $V_{ref} = V_{dd}/2$.
2. Activation connects a cell capacitor to the bitline, causing charge sharing and a small voltage shift.
3. Sensing amplifies that differential signal and drives the complementary bitlines toward $V_{dd}$ and $V_{ss}$.
4. Restoration writes the fully amplified value back to the cell.

This process depends on non-ideal effects. Word lines behave like distributed transmission structures rather than ideal wires. Bitlines carry parasitic resistance and capacitance. The sense path sees load not only from the cell array but also from local and global I/O structures. Some designs also use overdriven sensing, briefly applying a higher internal voltage to accelerate latching. All of these affect how quickly the row’s value becomes stable and how much slack truly exists inside $t_{RAS}$.

Without a realistic model of these effects, it is easy to assume there is more opportunity for multiplexing than the hardware can actually support.

## Activation-Coupled Refresh as a Protection Strategy

The key architectural idea is to decouple data holding from refresh execution. If the value of the currently active row can be latched into a small buffer, then the original bitline sensing path can be temporarily reused for a different purpose before the row is closed.

One way to implement this is to introduce a secondary buffering sense amplifier alongside the original sense amplifier. The original path still performs the initial sensing. The buffer then holds the activated row’s value while the bitline is temporarily disconnected, precharged, and reassigned to another row. This opens a window in which a shadow row can be refreshed in parallel with the logical lifetime of the original row.

This is not merely a convenience. It changes the role of an activation. Instead of an `ACT` command opening one row and doing only one unit of useful work, the activation becomes a trigger for both normal access and internal preventative maintenance.

The operation can be described in three stages.

In the first stage, the requested row is activated and its value is latched. The buffering amplifier preserves that value even after the bitline is detached.

In the second stage, the bitline is precharged and reused to open a shadow row. The original sense amplifier now refreshes that shadow row while the buffered data of the requested row remains intact.

In the third stage, the shadow operation completes, the bitline is prepared again, and the requested row is reconnected so its own full restoration can finish.

The reason this works architecturally is that the row remains logically open from the controller’s perspective. Reads and writes still target the buffered representation of the active row, so the external DDR interface does not need to change.

## Scaling Protection With $t_{RAS}$ Slack

Once a single shadow refresh can be overlapped with one activation, the next design question is how many such refreshes can be issued before timing becomes unsafe. A convenient parameter for this is `V`, the number of shadow rows refreshed per activation.

The timing relationship can be expressed as

$$
t_{RAS} \geq 32 + (V - 1) \times 17.5 \text{ ns}
$$

This formula captures the core tradeoff. With `V = 1`, the mechanism fits into the standard 32 ns active window. Larger values of `V` require longer active time, because each additional shadow refresh consumes extra internal precharge and sensing time. But that longer active time buys stronger protection, since more restorative work is being issued per activation.

This is a useful architectural knob. A device with a relatively high Rowhammer threshold may only need `V = 1`, while a more fragile device can increase `V` and accept some additional timing or performance cost in exchange for better protection.

## A Stateless Cyclic Refresh Policy

Hardware support for parallel refresh is only one part of the problem. A protection policy still has to decide when and where those extra refreshes go.

A simple and robust option is a cyclic policy within each sub-array. Instead of tracking aggressor rows, the DRAM keeps a lightweight cursor over the rows of the sub-array. Every `T` activations to that sub-array, it refreshes `V` rows starting from the current cursor position, then advances the cursor. Over time, the mechanism walks through the entire sub-array in order.

This kind of policy is attractive because it is largely insensitive to attacker behavior. An adversary can change access order, stride pattern, or aggressor location, but the refresh schedule continues to advance. Protection no longer depends on whether the device correctly guessed which row was dangerous.

The associated bound on maximum hammerings is

$$
H_{max} = \frac{N_{rows}}{V} \times (T + 1) + B
$$

where $N_{rows}$ is the number of rows in the sub-array, `V` is the number of shadow rows refreshed per trigger, `T` is the activation interval between refresh events, and `B` is the blast diameter.

The interpretation is straightforward: $H_{max}$ is the largest number of times a victim row can be exposed before the cyclic schedule is guaranteed to reach it. As long as

$$
H_{max} < R_{thresh}
$$

the device remains protected.

This is an important shift in how the defense is reasoned about. The problem is no longer framed as “detect the attacker before it is too late.” It becomes “guarantee that every vulnerable row is revisited often enough, regardless of who the attacker chooses to hammer.”

## Measuring Timing Slack in Practice

A natural concern is whether there is enough real slack inside $t_{RAS}$ to support this kind of internal multiplexing. One direct way to test that is to shorten $t_{RAS}$ experimentally and measure whether read-back corruption appears.

The methodology can be summarized as follows:

<details><summary>Code</summary>

```text
S <- PICKRANDOMROWS(1000)
for t <- {8, 16, 24, 32} do
  for R <- S do
    Fill R with a random data pattern
    Send ACT-PRE to R with tRAS = t
    Read R
    C^t_R <- number of corruptions in R
    if C^t_R == 0 then
      Remove R from S
return C
```

</details>

The logic is simple. If a row can be activated and precharged with a reduced active time and still read back correctly, then the nominal specification contains slack that can potentially be reused for internal work. Reported results on modern DDR4 and DDR5 devices show no corruption even at 16 ns in the tested setups, suggesting that the standard 32 ns active window is conservative enough to accommodate a shadow refresh in practice.

Equally important, shortening the active time in these tests does not significantly hurt retention behavior. That matters because a design that protects against Rowhammer by silently weakening ordinary charge restoration would not be acceptable.

## Hardware Cost and Implementation Structure

An activation-coupled protection mechanism of this type adds circuitry, but not much control complexity. The major hardware additions are buffering sense amplifiers and the gating required to isolate them during shadow operations. The policy logic itself can remain small: a per-sub-array row pointer and an activation counter are enough to drive a cyclic refresh schedule.

That asymmetry is valuable. The mechanism adds some analog or mixed-signal hardware near the sensing path, but it avoids large tracking tables, associative lookups, or sophisticated attacker-sensitive heuristics. In practice, this means area overhead can stay low while predictability remains high.

Another practical benefit is standards compatibility. Because the extra refresh work is hidden inside the timing of an ordinary activation, the external DDR protocol does not need new commands. That lowers the barrier to deployment compared with solutions that require interface changes or controller-DRAM coordination beyond existing timing semantics.

## Reliability, Performance, and Energy Tradeoffs

Any in-DRAM architectural change has to be evaluated along three axes: does it still restore charge reliably, what does it do to performance, and how much energy does it cost?

Reliability depends on whether the sensing and buffering circuitry remains robust under process variation. Monte Carlo analysis is a common way to evaluate that, because it simulates random transistor and parasitic variation across many instances of the circuit. Results in this design space suggest that the sensing path can remain reliable even with realistic variation, which is a prerequisite for trusting the entire mitigation strategy.

Performance overhead depends strongly on `V`. When `V = 1`, the extra work can often fit inside existing timing slack and therefore impose little to no visible system overhead. As `V` grows, the active time must be extended, and the cost becomes more visible at the system level. This is the intended tradeoff: stronger protection against lower $R_{thresh}$ values costs more time per activation.

Energy overhead also rises because each activation triggers additional internal precharge and sensing operations. A representative per-activation characterization is

```math
E_{overhead}(V) \in \{38\%, 95\%, 223\%, 479\%\}
\text{ for }
V \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}
```

This increase is significant, but it is also interpretable. The system is explicitly spending more energy on prevention because it is doing more work on every activation. The value of the architecture is that this extra energy buys deterministic protection rather than a statistical chance of avoiding failure.

## Why Activation-Coupled Refresh Matters

The most important idea here is not a single named mechanism, but a broader design pattern for future Rowhammer defenses.

Instead of building ever more complicated tracking logic, the memory device can use internal buffering and timing slack to convert ordinary activations into refresh opportunities. Instead of predicting which rows are hot, it can use a cyclic schedule that guarantees coverage at a configurable rate. Instead of assuming the blast diameter will stay fixed, it can incorporate `B` directly into the protection bound and tighten `T` or raise `V` as devices become more vulnerable.

That combination of circuit awareness, low state, and configurable protection makes this class of mitigation especially appealing for future DRAM generations. It aligns the defense with the underlying hardware rather than layering a large policy engine on top of it.

# 2. Bit-Efficient ChipKill Using Residue Codes

Modern memory protection is not only about correcting errors. It is also about making room for security metadata and new system functions without adding more memory traffic. That is why bit-efficient ChipKill has become an architectural problem rather than a narrow coding-theory exercise.

Traditional ChipKill-class protection is often implemented with Reed-Solomon-style codes that tolerate a full-chip failure, but those schemes are rigid. They consume a fixed number of redundancy bits, and once those bits are committed to reliability there is little room left for inline metadata such as memory tags, Rowhammer-detection state, or near-memory computation support. The important question is no longer just "can the code correct a chip failure?" It is "can it do so while leaving some space behind?"

## Why Bit Efficiency Matters in Modern Memory

The design problem begins from a shift in what memory protection is expected to do. In older systems, ECC primarily targeted random bit errors. In modern DRAM, protection must also account for whole-device failures, on-die transformations, and security features that want metadata close to the data path. Every redundant bit has therefore become contested space.

ChipKill is the clearest example. Its goal is to tolerate the complete failure of one DRAM chip, which means the code has to correct a full symbol rather than an isolated bit. That is much stronger than SECDED, but it usually costs more redundancy. If those bits are consumed entirely by reliability, there is no easy place to store ARM MTE tags, Rowhammer hashes, or similar inline metadata. The system then falls back to separate metadata regions, which consume bandwidth and energy.

The key idea here is that the coding scheme itself can be redesigned to recover some of that lost space. The contribution is not only a better ECC point on a coding-theory chart. It is a storage-security co-design argument: if the memory code becomes more bit-efficient, the saved bits can support other system goals.

## How Residue-Based Protection Works

Residue codes come from arithmetic error detection. The basic idea is simple: choose a constant multiplier `m`, encode the data so the final codeword has a known remainder relationship with `m`, and then check that relationship on read. A non-zero residue becomes the syndrome that identifies the error.

The naive formulation is:

$$
codeword = m \times data
$$

$$
remainder = codeword \bmod m
$$

$$
data =
\begin{cases}
codeword/m, & \text{remainder} = 0 \\
\frac{codeword - f_{error}(remainder)}{m}, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

This version explains the logic clearly. If the retrieved codeword is divisible by `m`, it is assumed to be correct. If not, the remainder is looked up in an error table, the corresponding error value is removed, and the original data is recovered by division.

That direct formulation is not attractive for memory because division on the read path is too slow. A practical implementation therefore uses a systematic version of residue coding, following Chien's formulation:

$$
codeword = (data \ll r) + X
$$

$$
X = m - ((data \ll r) \bmod m)
$$

Here `r` is the number of redundancy bits and `X` is the check field appended to the shifted data. The important architectural point is that the original data remains intact in the codeword. That means the processor can access the data bits immediately while the memory controller checks the modulo condition in parallel. In the common no-error case, the code behaves like a zero-added-latency checker.

This systematic form is the first reason residue codes become plausible for memory again. They no longer force an expensive arithmetic decode on every clean read. It also introduces a practical design constraint: if `X` has to fit in `r` bits, then the multiplier must satisfy $m < 2^r$.

## Why Symbol-Level Faults Drive the Design

Getting residue codes to correct a single flipped bit is not the hard part. The hard part is modern DRAM failure granularity. ChipKill is about symbol errors: a symbol corresponds to the bits contributed by one DRAM device, so a single chip fault can corrupt several bits at once.

That changes the search problem completely. For single-bit correction, each position needs a unique non-zero remainder for the two signed error values $+2^i$ and $-2^i$. For symbol correction, the code must distinguish every possible non-zero error pattern within the symbol. The number of possible error values grows exponentially with symbol width, which makes it much harder to find a small multiplier `m` that keeps all remainders unique.

This is where older views of residue codes ran into trouble. They were often dismissed as good for arithmetic but poor for practical storage codes because the required moduli became too large or too awkward. The key move is to show that the obstacle is not residue coding itself. The obstacle is the mapping from logical bits to physical symbols.

## The Core Idea: Shuffle Bits Before Symbol Coding

Symbol-level residue codes become practical through two ideas: symbol-aware error modeling and bit shuffling.

The symbol-aware part is conceptually direct. Instead of reasoning about isolated bit flips, the design enumerates the numerical error values produced by all error patterns within a DRAM chip and asks whether their residues modulo `m` are unique. If they are, the syndrome still identifies the failing pattern.

The shuffling part is what makes this search succeed. Logical bit order does not have to match physical chip placement. By permuting which codeword bits land in which symbol, the design changes the numerical values associated with symbol errors. That reshapes the residue space and can turn an impossible search into a successful one.

This is a subtle but powerful idea. The code is not only choosing a multiplier. It is co-designing the arithmetic code and the physical bit placement. In other words, wiring discipline becomes part of the coding strategy.

Valid codes are found through an exhaustive design-time search. For a chosen code length, redundancy width, and symbol size, the search tests candidate odd multipliers and checks whether every relevant symbol-error pattern produces a unique non-zero remainder. The search is expensive, but it happens offline. Runtime hardware only needs the winning multiplier and shuffle pattern.

The search process can be summarized as:

<details><summary>Code</summary>

```cpp
input : r redundancy bits, s byte size, n code length
output: list of valid multipliers mults

S = assignBitsToSymbols(s, n);
mults = empty();
R = remaindersNeeded(s, n);

for odd m from 2^r + 1 to 2^(r+1) - 1 do
  remSet = empty();
  for Si in S do
    for errPattern from 1 to 2^s - 1 do
      for errVal in getErrVals(errPattern, Si) do
        remSet.insert(errVal mod m);
  if remSet.size() == R && 0 not in remSet then
    mults.insert(m);
```

</details>

This code example matters because it captures the real design boundary. The expensive part is not runtime correction. The expensive part is the one-time search for a multiplier and shuffle that make the syndrome space unique.

## Residue-Code Layouts for DDR4 and DDR5

Several concrete code constructions make the argument clear for real memory organizations.

For DDR4-style 144-bit channels, a 144,132 configuration provides ChipKill protection with 12 check bits, whereas a comparable Reed-Solomon construction would use 16. That reduction is the core bit-efficiency result: the same failure model, fewer ECC bits.

For DDR5-style 80-bit codewords, an 80,69 configuration leaves five bits available for metadata after providing the required protection. That is strategically important because those spare bits are exactly what allow inline security features without extra memory requests.

More specialized cases show the same flexibility. An 80,70 hybrid code can cover both 4-bit asymmetric errors and single-bit bidirectional errors. An 80,67 design demonstrates that shuffling is not cosmetic: sequential bit placement fails to find any usable multiplier below the available redundancy bound, but a carefully chosen 8-bit shuffle succeeds.

One representative shuffle pattern is:

$$
S_{i \in [0,9]} = [b_i, b_{10+i}, b_{20+i}, b_{30+i}, b_{40+i}, b_{50+i}, b_{60+i}, b_{70+i}]
$$

Instead of grouping adjacent logical bits into one chip, the design spreads them across the word in a regular stride. That changes the arithmetic signature of each chip failure and creates a workable residue code where naive placement cannot.

The hybrid layout uses a second structured shuffle:

$$
S_{2 \times i} = [b_i, b_{10+i}, b_{20+i}, b_{30+i}], \quad i \in [0,9]
$$

$$
S_{2 \times i+1} = [b_{40+i}, b_{50+i}, b_{60+i}, b_{70+i}], \quad i \in [0,9]
$$

This arrangement distributes bits across symbols in an interleaved pattern and supports a mixed protection model that can simultaneously cover 4-bit asymmetric errors and any 1-bit bidirectional error.

This flexibility is something Reed-Solomon does not offer easily. Traditional storage codes usually come with fixed symbol structures and coarse parameter steps. This approach can tune the protection point at single-bit granularity, which is precisely why it can free a few extra bits for other purposes.

## Why Asymmetric Error Models Matter

The design also considers asymmetric error models, where cells are assumed to fail mainly as `1 -> 0` rather than in both directions. This matters in DRAM-like settings where reduced refresh or certain disturbance behaviors make charge loss the dominant mechanism.

An asymmetric model shrinks the space of error patterns the code must distinguish. That can improve efficiency further, and it helps explain how hybrid codes can cover different failure types simultaneously. The broader point is that residue coding is flexible enough to match the actual physics of the memory technology rather than forcing every platform into a symmetric bit-flip abstraction.

## Making Arithmetic ECC Practical in the Controller

Bit efficiency would not matter if the implementation were too slow. The obvious criticism of residue codes is that modulo arithmetic sounds expensive compared with XOR trees. The architecture answers that concern at the circuit level.

The underlying bottleneck is the naive modulo definition:

$$
r = c - m \times \lfloor c/m \rfloor
$$

The formula is simple, but the division term $\lfloor c/m \rfloor$ is what makes a direct hardware implementation too slow. Generic division can take tens of cycles, which is unacceptable on a memory-controller hot path.

The design therefore uses a systematic decoder, so clean reads can expose data immediately while the residue check runs in parallel. For the arithmetic itself, it avoids general-purpose division and instead uses constant-specific hardware. The modulo operation is derived from multiplication by the fixed inverse of `m`, following the same general logic that Granlund-Montgomery and Lemire-style methods use to replace division by constants with multiplications and shifts.

Because the multiplier is fixed, the hardware can be specialized aggressively. Booth encoding reduces partial products, Wallace trees sum them with low depth, and the tree can be pruned further because many partial products are known to be zero for the chosen constant. This is not a generic arithmetic unit. It is a code-specific datapath built around one residue modulus.

The outcome is that both encoding and correction complete in about three cycles. That is still more arithmetic than Reed-Solomon's XOR-heavy logic, and the area cost is real. But the latency is low enough that the scheme becomes practical, especially because the common error-free read path does not wait on full correction.

## What the Bit Savings Enable

The architecture becomes especially interesting once ECC bits stop being treated as reliability-only overhead. The recovered bits are used as inline storage for additional system functions.

The first use case is memory tagging. ARM MTE and similar schemes attach small tags to pointers and memory allocations so that tag mismatches expose bugs such as use-after-free and out-of-bounds accesses. Conventional designs often store tags in separate DRAM regions or shadow structures, which consumes bandwidth and power. The scheme can embed those tags directly inside the memory codeword when enough bits are saved, eliminating those extra accesses.

The second use case is Rowhammer detection. One option is to use the spare bits to store a 40-bit cryptographic hash per cache line so that malicious disturbance-induced changes become detectable with about $2^{-40}$ collision probability. The broader point is not that hashes solve Rowhammer alone, but that bit-efficient ECC creates room for security metadata near the data path, where it is most useful.

The third use case is processing in memory. Residue codes have a homomorphic property for arithmetic:

$$
e(f(x, y)) = f(e(x), e(y))
$$

That property is unusually attractive for PIM systems because the same code family can protect data while it is stored and while it is being manipulated arithmetically inside the memory device. In PIM-enabled HBM2-like settings, the same approach could use 2.6x fewer bits than current alternatives while protecting both storage and computation with one code family.

## Why Bit-Efficient ChipKill Matters

The deeper contribution is to recover a branch of coding theory that memory systems largely set aside decades ago. Residue codes were once associated with arithmetic reliability and fault-tolerant computers, not with mainstream DRAM protection. The common assumption was that they were too serial, too division-heavy, or too awkward for storage.

This architecture shows that those objections depend on old implementation assumptions. Once constant-division tricks, systematic encoding, and code-aware shuffling are brought together, residue codes become a serious alternative for modern memories. More importantly, they solve a problem that traditional ECC does not solve cleanly: how to preserve strong device-level protection while leaving room for security metadata and future near-memory computation.

This is why the approach feels timely. DDR5, on-die ECC, Rowhammer, memory tagging, and PIM all push memory design toward tighter integration between reliability and security. In that environment, the best code is not merely the one that corrects errors. It is the one that corrects errors while leaving architectural freedom for everything else the system now needs.

# 3. Open and Inspectable Roots of Trust

The same architectural mindset appears at the platform trust layer. OpenTitan exposes the structure of a real silicon root of trust to technical inspection. The interesting question is not whether secure boot exists in the abstract, but whether the boot chain, key hierarchy, lifecycle states, fault responses, and verification process form a coherent trust model.

A root of trust has to do more than verify a signature. It has to authenticate code before execution, derive identities and secrets in a controlled way, contain high-value key material, handle ownership transfer, react to physical tampering, and remain sound across manufacturing, deployment, service, and retirement. OpenTitan treats those requirements as architectural boundaries rather than firmware conventions.

## Architectural Foundations

The open portion covers the security architecture that determines who can boot code, how keys are derived, how ownership changes, how alerts propagate, and how security-critical blocks interact. The proprietary portion lies mainly in manufacturing-dependent analog details and physical hardening that are necessary for a commercial chip but do not need to obscure the logical trust model.

That split is important because it separates two very different questions. The first is whether the security design is coherent and auditable. The second is whether a specific manufacturing flow can deliver the physical resistance needed for a product. By keeping the logic open, the architecture exposes the trust assumptions. By allowing implementation-specific hardening, it remains realistic about what semiconductor production actually requires.

## Boot Flow and Identity Derivation

The identity architecture follows a DICE-style model in which each boot stage measures the next stage and uses those measurements to derive new identities and secrets. This avoids relying on a single static device credential. Instead, the device can attest not only to the underlying hardware root but also to the exact software state that was loaded through the chain.

Two identity domains are especially important. The creator domain anchors the immutable portion of the device and establishes that the silicon and early trust base are genuine. The owner domain sits above that foundation and allows the deployed system to assert control with its own keys and software policy. This division makes it possible to keep the foundational trust logic inspectable while still allowing different downstream operators to run distinct software stacks.

The key point is that identity derivation is not treated as a software convention. It is implemented as a hardware-governed process tied to root secrets and measured state. That design reduces the amount of early boot software that must be trusted with high-value material and narrows the opportunities for key exposure during initialization.

## Key Management and Secret Containment

A major technical strength of the design is the decision to isolate key management from the general-purpose processor. Root secrets and key manager state are not simply left in ordinary memory for firmware to manipulate carefully. Instead, dedicated hardware derives and stages keys according to lifecycle and boot state, while sensitive material is delivered directly to cryptographic engines through side-loading paths.

That side-loading model matters because it changes the failure mode of a software compromise. If the CPU never directly handles certain keys, then a compromised boot stage or operating environment is less likely to become a complete secret-extraction event. The processor can request cryptographic services, but the raw key material remains confined to hardware boundaries that are narrower and easier to reason about than the full software stack.

The design also distinguishes between attestation-oriented derivation and sealing-oriented derivation. The attestation chain produces identities bound to measured software state, which is useful for proving what has booted. The sealing chain produces stable secrets for storage protection and application use cases where continuity matters more than measurement freshness. Keeping those functions separate avoids overloading one key hierarchy with incompatible requirements.

## Ownership Transfer and Trust Transition

One of the more unusual design choices is the way control moves from the creator domain to the deploying owner. Rather than forcing long-term dependence on the original vendor, the architecture supports an ownership transfer flow in which a new owner verifies device authenticity, installs a new signing authority, boots owner-controlled software, and then provisions deployment-specific secrets.

Technically, this is significant because it avoids a dual-signing model in which both the original manufacturer and the current operator must approve executable state. That kind of arrangement can simplify vendor control, but it complicates sovereignty and introduces long-lived external dependencies into the boot policy. Here, the immutable layer authenticates the hardware and initial trust base, but it does not permanently monopolize authority over the mutable software stack.

The unlock state is also important. When ownership is relinquished, the platform can clear secrets and return to a state where a new owner can claim it without endorsement from the previous one. This is a clean hardware-supported mechanism for late binding. The same silicon can be manufactured once and deployed into different trust environments later, without baking all downstream trust decisions into factory provisioning.

## Lifecycle State as a Security Boundary

Lifecycle control is more than a management feature. It is one of the main security boundaries in the system. Manufacturing, testing, deployment, RMA, and retirement are not treated as informal operational modes; they are encoded as hardware states with explicit transition rules and restrictions on which secrets, debug pathways, and provisioning interfaces remain available.

This matters because many real attacks happen across lifecycle boundaries rather than inside a single boot session. A device that was safe in production can become weak if test access is not fully closed, if manufacturing secrets remain reachable, or if exceptional states are reversible in unsafe ways. Hardware-enforced lifecycle transitions reduce that risk by making certain moves one-way. Once the system leaves an earlier stage, the attack surface associated with that stage can be irreversibly cut off.

In practical terms, lifecycle control also simplifies reasoning about key validity. A key can be accepted or rejected not just based on metadata in software, but based on whether the hardware state machine says that operation is even legal in the current phase of the device's life.

## Cryptographic Offload With OTBN

Asymmetric cryptography is handled by a dedicated big-number engine rather than by the main CPU alone. That coprocessor is built for wide arithmetic operations such as modular exponentiation and elliptic-curve routines, which are performance-sensitive and easy to get subtly wrong. Offloading them into a specialized engine improves both performance isolation and assurance structure.

The assurance gain comes from the fact that critical routines can be specified, tested, and formally verified at the engine level. For arithmetic-heavy primitives, ordinary regression testing is not enough. Edge cases in modular reduction, exponentiation flow, or error handling can create exploitable deviations even when the implementation appears to work under normal tests. Formal analysis gives stronger evidence that the implemented algorithms match their intended behavior across the full state space that matters.

This engine also benefits from the same secret-containment philosophy seen elsewhere. When key material is delivered directly into cryptographic hardware, the system reduces the number of places where highly sensitive operands exist in software-visible form.

## Defenses Against Fault Injection and Side Channels

A secure root of trust has to assume an attacker may target the chip physically rather than only through software inputs. The architecture reflects that assumption in several ways. One of the clearest examples is dual-lockstep execution for the main processor. Two cores execute the same instruction stream, and their outputs are compared. A mismatch indicates that one path may have been perturbed by a transient fault such as a voltage glitch, clock manipulation, or injected optical disturbance.

This is a costly design choice, but it directly addresses a realistic class of attacks against secure boot and privilege transitions. If an attacker can induce the processor to skip a branch, miscompute a comparison, or mis-handle an exception at exactly the right moment, purely software defenses can fail silently. Lockstep comparison converts some of those silent failures into explicit hardware-detectable events.

The rest of the platform applies similar thinking. Sparse finite-state machine encodings make it harder for faults to push control logic into valid but unintended states. Redundant encodings on sensitive signals reduce the chance that a single perturbation cleanly flips system behavior. Masked implementations in cryptographic blocks help reduce side-channel leakage, especially in algorithms where intermediate values can correlate strongly with key bits.

## Alert Handling and System Response

Detection alone is not enough; the system also needs a coherent response path. That is the role of the alert handler. Instead of letting each block define its own isolated reaction to anomalies, the architecture gathers alerts from cryptographic units, logic monitors, and physical sensors into a centralized response mechanism.

This improves security in two ways. First, it creates a consistent policy layer for escalation, logging, throttling, or terminal response. Second, it allows different kinds of suspicious behavior to be combined rather than evaluated one by one in isolation. A single minor signal may not justify a drastic reaction, but correlated anomalies across clocking, voltage, and computation paths can indicate active tampering and should be treated as such.

In complex security chips, centralized response logic is often as important as the sensors themselves. Without it, a design may detect many interesting conditions but fail to turn them into a reliable security posture.

## Memory Protection and Data Scrambling

The memory system is designed with the assumption that physical access is in scope. SRAM and flash contents cannot be treated as naturally private simply because they are on-chip. As a result, integrity protection and scrambling mechanisms are used so that a raw memory dump is less likely to reveal usable secrets or code images directly.

This is particularly important for a trust anchor, where small amounts of extracted data can be disproportionately valuable. Boot measurements, sealed values, and key-related metadata all become higher-value targets once an attacker has physical possession of the device. Scrambling and integrity protection do not eliminate that risk by themselves, but they force attacks away from trivial readout and toward more complex compromise paths.

## Verification as a Security Discipline

The technical story is incomplete without verification. A root of trust is only as credible as the evidence behind its implementation. That is why the platform uses a staged lifecycle for IP maturity, strong coverage requirements, continuous integration, and strict review processes. These are not project-management details; they are part of the security case.

For hardware, the gap between "implemented" and "trusted" is large. RTL can compile and pass directed tests while still hiding specification mismatches, unreachable states, or insufficiently explored corner cases. Coverage targets force the team to demonstrate that intended behaviors and edge conditions have actually been exercised. Formal work on selected cryptographic routines complements simulation by proving properties that tests alone cannot establish convincingly.

The development process also favors small, reviewable changes and a high bar for direct commit access. In a security-critical hardware codebase, control over how modifications enter the tree is itself a security mechanism. The fewer opaque changes that slip through, the easier it is to preserve confidence in the design as it evolves.

## Why an Inspectable Root of Trust Matters

What makes this design technically notable is not any single block in isolation. It is the way the pieces reinforce one another. Measured boot feeds identity derivation. Identity derivation feeds attestation and owner control. Lifecycle state constrains which secrets and interfaces are valid. Key isolation reduces the blast radius of software compromise. Lockstep execution and alert handling turn physical perturbations into detectable events. Verification discipline makes the entire stack more defensible.

---

These five directions are all responding to the same underlying reality: modern hardware security breaks down when it relies on average-case behavior, hidden margins, or implicit trust.

The stronger designs are the ones that make the dangerous quantity explicit and then bind it to hardware action. In Rowhammer tracking, the dangerous quantity is hot-row frequency inside a bounded window. In hybrid tracking, it is the gap between a tiny physical threshold and a huge activation budget. In activation-coupled refresh, it is the number of shadow rows refreshed per activation and the maximum hammering exposure before coverage returns. In bit-efficient ChipKill, it is the redundancy budget that has to serve reliability and security at once. In OpenTitan, it is the trust state of the machine across boot, lifecycle, and attack response.
